Good nutrition is the foundation of your pet's health. With so many food options on the market, knowing what to look for — and what to avoid — can feel overwhelming. Here's your beginner's guide to pet nutrition.

## Reading the Label

Always check that a named meat source (chicken, beef, salmon) appears as the first ingredient. Avoid foods that lead with generic terms like "meat meal" or "by-product meal" as the primary ingredient.

Look for the AAFCO statement on the label — this confirms the food meets minimum nutritional standards for your pet's life stage.

## Macronutrients

**Protein** is the most important macronutrient for both cats and dogs. It supports muscle development, immune function, and energy.

**Fats** provide concentrated energy and support brain health, skin, and coat condition. Omega-3 fatty acids (from fish oil or flaxseed) are particularly beneficial.

**Carbohydrates** provide fibre and energy. While cats have limited ability to digest carbs, moderate amounts are fine for dogs.

## Hydration

Many pets don't drink enough water, especially those eating dry kibble. Adding wet food to the diet or using a pet water fountain can significantly increase water intake — critical for kidney health, particularly in cats.

## Life Stage Feeding

Nutritional needs change throughout your pet's life:

- **Puppies and kittens** need calorie-dense food with extra protein and calcium
- **Adult pets** do best with balanced maintenance diets
- **Senior pets** benefit from reduced calories, higher fibre, and joint-supporting nutrients like glucosamine

## Foods to Avoid

Never feed your pet: chocolate, grapes, raisins, onions, garlic, xylitol (a sweetener), macadamia nuts, or alcohol. These are toxic and can be life-threatening.

When in doubt about a specific food, consult your veterinarian before offering it to your pet.
