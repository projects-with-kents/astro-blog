Spaying and neutering are among the most responsible decisions a pet owner can make. Beyond population control, these procedures offer significant health and behavioural benefits for your companion animal.

## Health Benefits

Spayed female pets have a dramatically reduced risk of uterine infections (pyometra) and breast cancer. Neutered males are protected from testicular cancer and have lower rates of prostate problems.

Pets that have been spayed or neutered also tend to live longer, healthier lives. The hormonal reduction decreases stress on the body and can reduce roaming behaviour that leads to injury.

## Behavioural Improvements

Intact animals are often driven by hormonal urges that can cause frustrating behaviours:

- **Roaming** — searching for a mate puts pets at risk of traffic and injury
- **Aggression** — especially in unneutered males
- **Marking** — territorial urine marking inside the home
- **Excessive vocalisation** — especially in cats in heat

Neutering eliminates or greatly reduces all of these behaviours.

## The Best Time to Spay or Neuter

For most dogs, the procedure is recommended between 6–12 months of age, though this can vary by breed and size. Cats can be spayed/neutered as early as 8 weeks in a shelter environment, though most vets recommend 4–6 months.

## Myths Debunked

**Myth:** "My pet will get fat after the procedure."
**Fact:** Weight gain is related to overfeeding and lack of exercise, not the surgery itself.

**Myth:** "I should let my female have one litter first."
**Fact:** There is no medical evidence to support this, and the risks of pregnancy can be significant.

Talk to your veterinarian to determine the best timing and approach for your specific pet.
